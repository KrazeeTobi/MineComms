I've got a few crafting ideas here. They might sound a bit iffy, maybe even stupid, but just hear me out. First there's the [B]Tube[/B], which is made from 6 Wood/Gold/Stone/Iron, arranged on the Workbench with 3 on the top and 3 on the bottom. The Tube can be used in many ways, most of them as crafting components. One such example is the [B]Telescope[/B], crafted by placing 1 Glass/Diamond on either side of the Tube, and can be used to observe things from a long way away (a Diamond Telescope has a better zoom than the Glass Telescope), which can come in handy if you're scouting out new territories on the surface. Another example functions as a new weapon, which might garner a bit of controversy and/or didsain, but i'm hoping it won't. By placing a Wood block in the middle-left Workbench space, a Tube (of any strength, preferably Iron) on the middle-right space, and a Redstone Torch in the centre space, one can create the [B]Musket[/B], a primitive-yet-effective ranged weapon with higher accuracy than the Bow and Arrow (yeah, I know it's a gun, but just stay with me), yet deteriorates in condition similar to how most other Tools do when out through their paces, and like most other Tools, the Musket will last longer (be able to fire off more shots) depending on the grade of Tube used. So even though the Musket is better for long-distance ranged attacks, it'd be wise to either have spares handy if your equipped Musket breaks, or simply have a Bow as well just in case. Instead of using Arrows, like the Bow, the Musket uses [B]Shots[/B], which are crafted from Flint, Cloth and Gunpowder in a similar fashion to how one crafts Arrows. Yep, now you can use Gunpowder for more than just blowing up mountains, and you can use Cloth for more than just making Paintings. I hope these ideas are at least viable, and it'd be appreciated if I get feedback, since feedback is cool, but keep it constructive, since it's important to know what's good and what's bad about an idea, thus I can go back and make changes if something's wrong.
