Here it is: Minecraft is an indie game made by Swedish developer, Markus Alexej Persson. It is an exploration survival game where the player must build a house and survive waves of monsters while obtaining resources to make their house--or cave, or flying island, or volcano, or castle, et cetera--more awesome. It has a stunning minimalistic art style (the world is composed of millions of small cubes with 16-bit graphics on each face) that never gets old, but if it isn’t visually appealing to you, there are hundreds of different graphic packs to change the look of everything. The worlds are infinite and randomly generated, so you will never explore the same area twice. To begin, you will start with nothing but the clothes on your back, immersed in a deep landscape filled with biomes, small areas with different conditions ranging from desert to mountain to tundra. You can dig up dirt to make a small hut to wait out the night, but it is not advised to go out too far from your spawn point before setting up camp because the point where you spawn is where you will respawn again upon death. After a place of refuge from the monsters at night is established, mining for coal and iron is the next step. While it is somehow possible for a player to use their bare fists to break rocks, cutting (punching?) down trees and crafting makeshift tools is the ideal alternative. When a pickaxe has been created, go look for some minerals to make better tools and torches to light up your house, since monsters spawn in the dark. While your very first home might not be much of a looker, keep expanding on it and soon you may have created a huge towering spire, a complex network of underground tunnels, floating islands, or more. You might have run in to diamonds, gold, or even obsidian, a dark purple block that just so happens to be the toughest material in the game and is found where water meets lava, normally in the vast series of natural caverns and tunnels below the surface. Making a gate of obsidian with a 3 block tall and 2 block wide gap in the middle and then lighting it on fire will create a portal to The Nether, an underworld filled with lava and fire, and home to the most devastating monsters in the game: Ghasts. Ghasts are floating ghostlike creatures that spit exploding fireballs at you which can not only kill a player in a few hits but also destroy their exit portal from The Nether. But while it is dangerous, The Nether is also home to some very useful blocks. But what if that huge castle gets lonely? What if wandering the halls alone grows boring? Not a problem! Minecraft’s multiplayer, although buggy (Minecraft is still in it’s Alpha stage, so it’s not totally polished) is even more fun than singleplayer. Just enter a server IP address in the box, and you will join a server filled with castles and bases other players have made. Once in, you can help a friend with their house, or build your own fortress and declare war on other player’s. So whether you want to try and survive an onslaught of terrifying creatures, or make a skyscraping stone monolith to look upon the surrounding environment, Minecraft is loads of fun and never gets old. Right now it’s €9.95 euros, or about $14 USD, although this price will double when the game enters Beta. When this will happen is unknown, so if you’re interested head over to [url]www.minecraft.net[/url] and buy it now or try the free creative version, although it’s nowhere near as fun as the Alpha.
